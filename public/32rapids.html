Introduction to the RAPIDs Framework

The RAPIDs framework was developed by Bain & Company as a powerful tool for clarifying decision-making roles in organizations. RAPID stands for Recommend, Agree, Perform, Input, and Decide—the five key roles in effective decision processes. By explicitly defining these roles, RAPIDs creates clarity around who should be involved at each stage of decision-making, reducing confusion, accelerating decision velocity, and improving outcomes.

The Decision-Making Challenge

Organizations frequently struggle with decision-making effectiveness, leading to several common challenges:

  • Decision paralysis: Unclear accountability leading to delayed or deferred decisions
  • Decision by committee: Too many stakeholders with apparent veto power
  • Rubber stamps: Formal approvers without real authority or sufficient information
  • Unclear implementation ownership: Decisions made without clear execution responsibility
  • Stakeholder exclusion: Key perspectives missing from the decision process

The RAPIDs framework addresses these challenges by creating a structured approach to decision rights. Unlike task-oriented frameworks like RACI, RAPIDs is specifically designed for decision processes, making it particularly valuable for strategic, cross-functional, and high-impact decisions that involve multiple stakeholders but require clear accountability.

The RAPIDs Framework Overview
R
Recommend

The person or group responsible for making a proposal, gathering input, analyzing data, and providing the rationale for a decision.

  • Initiates the decision process
  • Creates the detailed proposal or recommendation
  • Gathers relevant data and input
  • Considers alternatives and performs analysis
  • Presents recommendation to the decision-maker
D
Decide

The single person who makes the final decision and is ultimately accountable for its outcome.

  • Has ultimate decision-making authority
  • Commits resources and approves actions
  • Resolves conflicts or disagreements
  • Ensures decision aligns with strategy
  • Takes responsibility for outcomes
A
Agree

Stakeholders who must formally approve the recommendation before final decision.

  • Has veto power or sign-off rights
  • Typically represents critical functions
  • Often legal, finance, or compliance roles
  • Can block recommendations if needed
  • Must be used sparingly to be effective
I
Input

People consulted for their views and expertise before a recommendation is developed.

  • Provides subject-matter expertise
  • Offers relevant perspectives
  • Consulted before recommendation is finalized
  • No veto power over recommendation
  • Views should be given serious consideration
P
Perform

Individuals or groups responsible for implementing the decision once it's made.

  • Executes the decision
  • Translates decision into action
  • May not be involved in making the decision
  • Should understand rationale behind decision
  • Provides feedback on implementation

The five RAPID components create a comprehensive framework for effective decision-making

Key RAPIDs Principles

Core Principles for Effective RAPIDs Implementation

The RAPIDs framework is guided by several fundamental principles that ensure clear decision rights and effective decision processes in organizations.

Decision Clarity Principles

  • Single Decision-Maker

    Every decision must have exactly one D role—the ultimate decision-maker who has final authority and accountability. This eliminates confusion and ensures clear ownership of the decision outcome.

  • Recommended Before Decided

    The R role should always complete their analysis and present a formal recommendation before the D makes the final decision. This ensures decisions are based on thorough analysis and options.

  • Limited Agree Roles

    The A role should be used sparingly and only for stakeholders with legitimate rights to veto or formal sign-off requirements. Too many A roles creates gridlock and delays decisions.

  • Separation of Roles

    While one person may play multiple roles in different decisions, within a single decision, roles should be clearly separated to ensure proper checks and balances in the decision process.

Process Effectiveness Principles

  • Input Before Recommendation

    Those with the I role should be consulted before the recommendation is finalized. This ensures diverse perspectives are considered and prevents last-minute objections that delay decisions.

  • Appropriate Input Scale

    The number of Input roles should be balanced—enough to gather necessary perspectives without creating excessive coordination complexity or delaying the recommendation.

  • Performers' Involvement

    Where possible, those with the P role should have some involvement earlier in the process—either as I or in developing parts of the R. This ensures better implementation of decisions once made.

  • Decision Scope Definition

    The specific decision being addressed must be clearly defined, including its scope, impact, and boundaries. Vague decision definitions lead to confusion in role assignments and process.

RAPIDs Implementation Principles
Decision-Making Authority
  • Match decision rights to formal authority
  • Align D roles with accountability level
  • Ensure decision-makers have needed resources
  • Connect authority to organizational strategy
  • Address gaps between formal and informal authority
Decision Process Design
  • Define clear decision process timelines
  • Document how input will be gathered
  • Create templates for recommendation format
  • Establish handoff protocols between roles
  • Define escalation paths for disagreements
Organizational Integration
  • Align with governance structures
  • Integrate with existing decision forums
  • Connect to meeting cadences and formats
  • Ensure consistency with org design
  • Enable cross-functional collaboration

Applying RAPIDs to Decision Types

Tailoring RAPIDs to Different Decision Categories

Not all decisions require the same level of rigor in applying the RAPIDs framework. By categorizing decisions based on their characteristics, you can apply the appropriate level of process formality and stakeholder involvement.

Decision Categorization Framework
Strategic Decisions

Major decisions with long-term impact that shape organizational direction:

  • Strategic investments and resource allocation
  • New market entry or exit decisions
  • Major organizational changes
  • Significant partnership or acquisition decisions
  • Long-term product roadmap decisions

RAPIDs Application: Full formal implementation with documented roles, structured process, multiple input sources, and senior executive involvement.

Cross-Functional Decisions

Decisions requiring coordination across multiple departments or functions:

  • Product launch decisions
  • Process changes affecting multiple departments
  • Resource allocation between departments
  • Customer experience initiatives
  • Technology platform decisions

RAPIDs Application: Structured framework with clear documentation of roles, formalized input process, and special attention to the "Agree" roles to manage cross-functional complexity.

Operational Decisions

Routine decisions focused on executing established strategies and processes:

  • Standard project approvals
  • Regular budget adjustments
  • Routine procurement decisions
  • Standard hiring decisions
  • Regular operational changes

RAPIDs Application: Simplified framework focusing primarily on clear R and D roles with streamlined input process and minimal formal documentation.

Decision Process Patterns
Centralized Decision Pattern
R: Subject experts
A: Limited/None
I: Key stakeholders
D: Senior leader
P: Implementation team

Best for: Major strategic decisions, significant resource allocation, crisis management, and decisions requiring strong alignment with corporate direction.

Consultative Decision Pattern
R: Project team
A: Key functions
I: Broad input
D: Department head
P: Cross-functional team

Best for: Cross-functional initiatives, organizational change decisions, policy changes, and decisions with broad stakeholder impact requiring buy-in.

Delegated Decision Pattern
R: Team member
A: None
I: Limited
D: Team leader
P: Same team

Best for: Routine operational decisions, tactical implementation choices, and decisions where speed and agility are more important than broad consensus.

Collaborative Decision Pattern
R: Core team
A: Partners
I: Diverse perspectives
D: Shared leadership
P: Extended team

Best for: Complex innovation decisions, product design choices, partnership arrangements, and decisions requiring creative problem-solving and diverse perspectives.

However, once a decision is made by the D, the implementation moves forward through the Perform step with clear accountability.

RAPIDs vs. RACI: Key Differences

Understanding Framework Differences and When to Use Each

While both RAPIDs and RACI are responsibility assignment frameworks, they serve different purposes and are optimized for different organizational needs. Understanding these differences helps in selecting the most appropriate framework for specific situations.

Comparative Framework Analysis
Characteristic RACI Framework RAPIDs Framework
Primary Focus Task and activity ownership across deliverables and processes Decision rights and decision-making processes
Chronological Aspect Generally static assignment of responsibilities without defined sequence Incorporates process flow with roles appearing in a typical sequence (Input → Recommend → Agree → Decide → Perform)
Accountability Structure One Accountable person per task who may delegate to Responsible parties One Decision-maker with separate roles for Recommenders and formal Approvers
Execution Focus Strong focus on those Responsible for doing the work (R role) Greater emphasis on who Recommends (R role) and Decides (D role), with implementation as a distinct later step (P role)
Stakeholder Distinctions Single Consulted role for all input providers Distinguishes between stakeholders providing Input (I) and those with formal Approval rights (A)
Implementation Scope Often applied broadly across many tasks and operational processes Typically applied to specific important decisions or decision categories
Visualization Format Usually presented as a matrix mapping roles to activities Often depicted as a process flow with decision rights at each stage
Intended Use Cases
  • Project management
  • Process definition
  • Cross-functional collaboration
  • Role clarity in operational activities
  • Strategic decision-making
  • Governance structures
  • Decision bottleneck resolution
  • Clarifying executive decision rights
When to Use RACI

  • Project Management

    When managing complex projects with many tasks and deliverables that need clear ownership assignment.

  • Process Documentation

    When defining standard operating procedures and recurring processes that will be executed repeatedly.

  • Task-Oriented Work

    When the focus is on execution of specific activities rather than decision-making.

  • Role Clarification

    When onboarding new team members or establishing clarity in matrix organizations about who does what.

  • Comprehensive Coverage

    When you need to map responsibilities across a wide range of activities in a department or function.

When to Use RAPIDs

  • Decision Process Improvement

    When organizations suffer from slow, unclear, or ineffective decision-making processes.

  • Governance Design

    When establishing or refining organizational governance structures and decision rights.

  • Strategic Decisions

    For high-impact decisions that require structured input, clear recommendations, and transparent approval processes.

  • Cross-Functional Collaboration

    When decisions require input and buy-in from multiple stakeholders across organizational boundaries.

  • Organizational Transformation

    During major change initiatives when decision processes need to be redesigned for greater effectiveness.

Practical Integration of RACI and RAPIDs

In many organizations, both frameworks can be used in complementary ways to address different aspects of organizational effectiveness:

Organizational Level RACI Application RAPIDs Application Integration Approach
Executive/Strategic Used for major strategic initiatives to define high-level responsibilities Applied to critical strategic and investment decisions with governance structures RAPIDs for decision governance framework with RACI for initiative execution
Business Unit/Department Used for process ownership and cross-functional role clarity Applied to key business decisions and resource allocation choices Departmental decision rights (RAPIDs) with process responsibilities (RACI)
Project Level Comprehensive task and deliverable responsibility matrix Framework for critical project decision points and stage gates Project governance model with RAPIDs for key decisions and RACI for workstreams
Operational/Process Detailed process steps and activity ownership Used only for critical operational decisions with significant impact RACI for standard operations with RAPIDs for exception handling and escalations

Key Integration Principle: Rather than viewing RACI and RAPIDs as competing frameworks, organizations achieve the best results by using them for their respective strengths. RAPIDs provides structure for how decisions are made, while RACI clarifies who is responsible for executing the work once decisions are made. Together, they create a comprehensive approach to organizational clarity.

Dell Case Study: RAPIDs for Strategic Platform Decisions

This example illustrates how Dell Technologies implemented the RAPIDs framework to improve decision-making for strategic technology platform investments across its global organization.

Challenge Context

Dell faced several decision-making challenges in its technology platform investments:

  • Slow decision cycles for strategic technology investments (6+ months)
  • Inconsistent stakeholder involvement across business units
  • Unclear accountability for final decisions amid matrix organization complexity
  • Significant resources spent on proposals that were ultimately rejected
  • Implementation delays due to late involvement of key stakeholders

Business Impact:

  • Missed market windows for competitive technology offerings
  • Inefficient use of engineering and product management resources
  • Inconsistent technology decisions across business units
  • Duplicate investments in similar capabilities
  • Difficulty tracking accountability for investment outcomes
RAPIDs Implementation Approach
Diagnostic Phase
  • Mapped current decision processes for technology investments
  • Identified key decision types and categorized by impact and frequency
  • Conducted stakeholder interviews to understand pain points
  • Analyzed historical decisions for patterns and bottlenecks
  • Benchmarked against industry best practices
Design Phase
  • Created decision rights framework using RAPIDs principles
  • Developed standardized templates for investment proposals
  • Established clear criteria for different investment tiers
  • Defined escalation paths for contentious decisions
  • Created governance structures aligned with decision rights
Implementation Phase
  • Conducted leadership training on RAPIDs principles
  • Piloted framework with several key technology decisions
  • Refined approach based on initial feedback
  • Created playbooks and reference materials
  • Established metrics to track decision effectiveness

Implementation Challenges
  • Cultural resistance: Senior leaders initially hesitant to relinquish perceived decision rights in their domains
  • Process adherence: Tendency to revert to previous informal decision-making during time pressure
  • Role confusion: Difficulty distinguishing between Input and Agree roles in practice
  • Timeliness concerns: Perception that formalized process would slow decisions further
  • Global coordination: Challenges implementing consistent process across regional teams
Success Factors
  • Executive sponsorship: CTO's active championship and personal modeling of the framework
  • Clear benefits case: Compelling data on cost of delayed decisions and rework
  • Practical tools: Simple templates, clear process guides, and decision tracking system
  • Flexibility: Tiered approach with lightweight process for smaller decisions
  • Celebration of success: Highlighting early wins and improved decision outcomes
Measurable Outcomes
  • Decision cycle time: Reduced from 6+ months to 10 weeks for major platform decisions
  • Implementation success: 35% improvement in on-time, on-budget delivery of approved investments
  • Resource efficiency: 28% reduction in engineering hours spent on proposals that weren't approved
  • Decision quality: 22% increase in technology investments meeting or exceeding ROI targets
  • Stakeholder satisfaction: 40-point improvement in internal survey scores on decision transparency
Key Learnings & Best Practices
  • Right-size the framework: Create tiered approach with appropriate rigor based on decision impact and investment size
  • Invest in education: Ensure all participants understand the purpose of each role and overall process flow
  • Start with pain points: Initially apply RAPIDs to decisions that have historically been most problematic
  • Focus on timelines: Set clear expectations for how long each stage should take to prevent delays
  • Measure effectiveness: Track decision velocity, quality, and adherence to process to demonstrate value

Sustainability Strategy: Dell incorporated RAPIDs into its standard technology governance framework and leadership development programs. The framework was expanded to other decision domains after demonstrating success in technology platforms. Regular reviews of decision effectiveness were established, and the process was refined based on ongoing feedback and changing business needs.

Implementing RAPIDs in Organizations

Implementation Strategies and Best Practices

Successfully implementing RAPIDs requires more than just defining roles and creating documentation. Organizations must address cultural factors, provide adequate support, and establish ongoing governance to ensure the framework delivers sustainable improvements in decision-making.

Implementation Phases
1. Assessment & Diagnosis
  • Identify current decision-making pain points
  • Map existing informal and formal decision processes
  • Catalog critical decisions that would benefit from clarity
  • Assess organizational readiness for change
  • Establish baseline metrics on decision effectiveness
2. Design & Development
  • Categorize decisions and develop appropriate RAPIDs models
  • Define clear roles for each decision category
  • Create supporting templates and documentation
  • Develop training and communication materials
  • Establish governance structure and measurement approach
3. Pilot Implementation
  • Select high-impact decisions for initial implementation
  • Train participants in pilot areas on framework
  • Provide coaching support during initial decision cycles
  • Gather feedback on process effectiveness
  • Refine approach based on pilot learnings
4. Scaling & Sustainability
  • Roll out framework to broader organization
  • Integrate with existing management systems
  • Create communities of practice for sharing best practices
  • Establish regular review cycles to refresh decision rights
  • Track and communicate impact and success stories
Critical Success Factors
Executive Sponsorship & Modeling

Leadership support and demonstration is essential:

  • Obtain visible endorsement from senior leadership
  • Ensure executives model framework in their own decisions
  • Address resistance at leadership level first
  • Connect framework to strategic priorities and values
  • Establish executive accountability for implementation
Change Management Approach

Thoughtful change management increases adoption:

  • Communicate compelling "why" for implementation
  • Address fear of loss of control or authority
  • Provide adequate training and support resources
  • Recognize and celebrate early adopters
  • Establish feedback mechanisms for continuous improvement
Practical Tools & Resources

Supporting materials enable consistent application:

  • Create clear templates for each RAPIDs role
  • Develop decision process playbooks and guides
  • Establish repositories for documentation
  • Provide facilitation resources for complex decisions
  • Leverage technology for tracking and transparency
Integration with Organizational Systems

Embedding RAPIDs into existing processes ensures sustainability:

  • Align with governance structures and meeting cadences
  • Incorporate into project management methodologies
  • Link to performance management and rewards
  • Include in onboarding for new leaders
  • Reference in strategic planning processes
Common Implementation Challenges
Cultural Resistance

Overcoming organizational inertia and existing power structures:

Challenge

  • Resistance to perceived loss of decision authority
  • Preference for informal influence networks
  • Skepticism about "another framework"

Mitigation Strategy

  • Focus on decision quality and speed benefits
  • Start with "coalition of the willing"
  • Showcase early successes and improvements
  • Address concerns through open dialogue
Complexity Management

Balancing comprehensiveness with usability:

Challenge

  • Over-engineering the framework
  • Creating excessive documentation
  • Applying heavy process to all decisions

Mitigation Strategy

  • Create tiered approach based on decision impact
  • Focus on high-value decisions initially
  • Develop simplified versions for routine decisions
  • Regularly refine to eliminate unnecessary steps
Accountability Enforcement

Ensuring adherence to defined roles:

Challenge

  • Reversion to old decision habits
  • Role confusion during implementation
  • Lack of consequences for non-adherence

Mitigation Strategy

  • Establish clear governance and oversight
  • Create transparency in decision process
  • Incorporate into leadership evaluations
  • Provide coaching during transition period
